Why this matters

Plans that haven't been tested aren't plans. They're documents.

An incident response plan is only as good as the team's ability to execute it under pressure. Communication breakdowns, unclear escalation paths, missing vendor contacts, decisions that look obvious on paper and paralyze people in a real event - these are the things tabletop exercises surface.

The goal isn't to run a perfect exercise. It's to find the gaps, have the hard conversations in a low-stakes environment, and leave with a clearer picture of what needs to change - and a score that shows where you actually stood.

📞
Communication breakdowns under pressure Who calls who? When? Who has authority to make containment decisions at 2am? Most plans assume this is obvious. It rarely is during an active incident.
⏱
Decision-making without a script Real incidents don't follow the scenario you planned for. Exercises build the muscle - so teams can adapt when the actual event looks different from the inject.
📋
Missing contacts and dependencies Cyber insurance contacts, legal counsel, regulators, third-party vendors - the people you need in an incident who aren't in your current runbook.
Methodology

Grounded in how attacks actually unfold

Scenarios aren't invented. They're built from the MITRE ATT&CK framework - a globally recognized knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques drawn from real-world incidents. Every scenario maps to specific attack stages: how the attacker got in, how they moved laterally, and what the impact looked like.

That grounding matters. It means your team is rehearsing responses to techniques that real threat actors use against organizations like yours - not hypotheticals someone made up in a boardroom.

The scenario is then contextualized to your environment: your vendors, your systems, your team structure, your regulatory obligations. The attack pattern is real. The situation is yours. After the exercise, the After Action Report maps your team's response to the relevant ATT&CK techniques - giving technical buyers and auditors a structured view of where the team performed well and where coverage was thin.

Gamified format

Scored, repeatable, and trackable over time

Most tabletop exercises produce a gap list and a thank-you email. This format produces a score - and a baseline you can measure against on the next run.

🎯

Decision point scoring

Each scenario includes structured decision points where participant responses are scored against a rubric: communication clarity, decision speed, process adherence, regulatory awareness. Not pass/fail - a calibrated score that shows where the team is strong and where the gaps are.

💉

Injects that raise the stakes

Mid-exercise injects introduce curveballs - a key person is unavailable, a vendor isn't responding, a second system goes down. These test adaptability, not just plan knowledge. Inject difficulty can be scaled between runs.

📈

Scores accumulate over time

Each engagement is a fresh scenario - different attack type, different injects, calibrated to your current threat environment and maturity. Run quarterly, the scores accumulate into a trend line that shows leadership whether IR capability is actually improving, not just whether the last exercise felt good.

Sample scenarios

What it looks like in your environment

Every scenario is written fresh for the client. These examples show how the same attack patterns play out differently depending on your operating environment.

🔐
Ransomware via phishing — files encrypted An employee clicked a link. Shared drives and M365 are encrypted. Backups haven't been tested. The insurance carrier is asking questions. Who makes the ransom call, and what do you need to answer them?
📧
BEC — wire transfer fraud Someone impersonating the owner emailed the bookkeeper with an urgent wire request. The transfer may have gone out. Who do you call first - the bank, your lawyer, or law enforcement - and what's the window to reverse it?
🔗
Accounting software breach notification Your cloud accounting provider sends a breach notification. Payroll records and banking details may be exposed. What are your obligations to employees, CRA, and your banking partners - and who handles each?
🏨
Ransomware hitting PMS at peak occupancy Your property management system is encrypted. Check-in is manual. OTA bookings are coming in that you can't process. The brand is calling. What happens in the first two hours?
💳
Payment card data breach Your payment processor flags unusual transaction patterns. PCI DSS breach notification timelines kick in. What are your obligations, to whom, and in what order?
🔗
OTA channel manager compromise A third-party channel manager has been compromised. Rates are being manipulated. Your credentials may be exposed. How do you respond when the attack vector isn't your system?
🌐
Multi-tenant blast radius via RMM A client environment is compromised through your RMM tooling. Other client environments may be affected. How do you isolate, notify, and manage relationships with six simultaneous stakeholders?
🛠
PSA / monitoring tool zero-day Your monitoring tool has a zero-day actively being exploited. 47 client environments may be exposed. Triage, notification, and communication sequence - what's the order, and who leads each stream?
📧
BEC targeting a client CFO A client's CFO responded to a targeted spear-phishing campaign. Wire transfer may be involved. You're managing the technical response and the client relationship at the same time.
🆔
Identity provider compromise — credential theft at scale An attacker has compromised your identity provider and is moving laterally using stolen credentials. How do you detect it, contain it, and scope the breach when everything looks like a legitimate login?
☁️
Cloud misconfiguration — sensitive data exposed A storage bucket has been publicly accessible. You don't know for how long or who accessed it. Client or employee data may be involved. What are your breach notification obligations and who leads the response?
👤
Insider threat — departing employee exfiltration HR flags unusual activity from an employee who gave notice last week. Large volumes of data moved to a personal cloud drive. What are your legal constraints around investigation, and when can you act?
Audience tracks

The right room for the right conversation

🖥

Operational (IT team)

Technical response scenarios - containment decisions, tool-specific procedures, escalation triggers, forensic preservation. Designed for the people who will be executing the response.

📊

Executive (C-suite & board)

Decision-making under uncertainty - ransom decisions, public disclosure, regulator notification, brand communication. For the people who own the business decisions, not the technical ones.

🤝

Vendor / third party

Coordinated response when the incident involves external parties. Who has access, who gets notified, who takes ownership - tested before a shared incident forces the question.

What you get

A scorecard and an After Action Report

Every exercise closes with two deliverables. The After Action Report documents what the exercise surfaced: gaps, decisions made well, decisions made poorly, and a prioritized improvement list. The Exercise Scorecard captures the scored results by category so leadership has a concrete baseline for the next run.

Both are working documents. The AAR goes to the people who need to fix things. The scorecard goes to leadership to show where the investment is moving the needle.

After Action Report includes

  • Scenario summary, MITRE ATT&CK technique mapping, and facilitation notes
  • Gap findings by category: communication, technical, process, regulatory
  • Inject response analysis - what worked, what broke
  • Prioritized IR plan improvement recommendations
  • Action item list with owners and suggested timelines

Exercise Scorecard includes

  • Five scored dimensions: Detection & Severity Assessment, Decision Authority & Escalation, Containment & Response Actions, Communication, Process & Documentation Adherence
  • 1–5 per dimension with facilitator notes; composite score = 0–100 with letter grade (A–F)
  • Quarter-over-quarter trend line across all completed exercises
  • AI Governance variant available with AI-specific scoring dimensions (Governance Awareness, Risk Assessment, Escalation, Stakeholder Communication, Regulatory Alignment)
