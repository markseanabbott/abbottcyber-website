Tabletop Exercises

Test your incident response plan before something forces you to use it

Most organizations discover the gaps in their incident response plan during an actual incident. A tabletop exercise finds them first - in a room, with the right people, under a realistic scenario built for your environment.

Book a tabletop exercise
Why this matters

Plans that haven't been tested aren't plans. They're documents.

An incident response plan is only as good as the team's ability to execute it under pressure. Communication breakdowns, unclear escalation paths, missing vendor contacts, decisions that look obvious on paper and paralyze people in a real event - these are the things tabletop exercises surface.

The goal isn't to run a perfect exercise. It's to find the gaps, have the hard conversations in a low-stakes environment, and leave with a clearer picture of what needs to change before something real happens.

Scenario design

Built for your environment, not a generic template

Hotels & Hospitality
🏨
Ransomware hitting PMS at peak occupancy Your property management system is encrypted. Check-in is manual. OTA bookings are coming in that you can't process. The brand is calling. What happens in the first two hours?
💳
Payment card data breach Your payment processor flags unusual transaction patterns. PCI DSS breach notification timelines kick in. What are your obligations, to whom, and in what order?
🔗
OTA channel manager compromise A third-party channel manager has been compromised. Rates are being manipulated. Your credentials may be exposed. How do you respond when the attack vector isn't your system?
MSP Partners
🌐
Multi-tenant blast radius A client environment is compromised via your RMM tooling. Other client environments may be affected. How do you isolate, notify, and manage relationships with six simultaneous stakeholders?
🛠
Compromised via MSP tooling Your PSA or monitoring tool has a zero-day actively being exploited. You have 47 client environments potentially exposed. Triage, notification, and communication sequence - what's the order?
📧
BEC targeting a client CFO A client's CFO has been receiving targeted spear-phishing and may have responded. Wire transfer may be involved. You're managing the technical response and the client relationship simultaneously.
Audience tracks

The right room for the right conversation

🖥

Operational (IT team)

Technical response scenarios - containment decisions, tool-specific procedures, escalation triggers, forensic preservation. Designed for the people who will be executing the response.

📊

Executive (C-suite & board)

Decision-making under uncertainty - ransom decisions, public disclosure, regulator notification, brand communication. For the people who own the business decisions, not the technical ones.

🤝

Vendor / third party

Coordinated response when the incident involves external parties - PMS vendors, OTA platforms, channel managers, cloud providers. Who has access, who gets notified, who takes ownership of what.

What you get

The After Action Report

Every tabletop exercise closes with an After Action Report - a structured summary of what the exercise surfaced: gaps identified, decisions made well, decisions made poorly, and a prioritized list of improvements to the IR plan before the next exercise or actual event.

The AAR is a working document, not a trophy. It goes to the people who need to fix things.

  • Exercise scenario summary and facilitation notes
  • Gap findings by category (communication, technical, process, regulatory)
  • Prioritized IR plan improvement recommendations
  • Action item list with owners and suggested timelines
  • Internal link to relevant resources and next steps
