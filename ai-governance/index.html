The problem

Fragmented AI use is a data exposure problem

Most organizations aren't running a coordinated AI strategy. They have people using individual ChatGPT accounts, pasting client data into free tools, and making ad hoc decisions about what's acceptable - with no policy, no oversight, and no visibility.

The most common AI risk isn't a sophisticated attack. It's an employee who summarized a confidential client proposal in a free consumer tool because no one told them not to. Whatever you put into commodity AI becomes part of the model's training data. Data classification isn't a compliance checkbox - it's the foundation.

AI readiness starts there. Before governance frameworks, before tool selection, before any deployment: what data do you have, and who's already sending it somewhere?

The most common governance mistake is treating all AI tools the same. A ChatGPT account carries different risk than M365 Copilot processing your email, which carries different risk again from an autonomous agent that writes code or modifies records without human review. Governing all three the same way leaves the highest-risk tools unaddressed.

AI Risk Framework

Three categories of AI risk. Most organizations govern only one.

Most AI governance programs focus on conversational AI - the ChatGPT problem. That's real, but it's only one tier. The tools that carry the most organizational risk are often the ones your people don't think of as AI at all.

💬

Tier 1: Conversational AI

ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Microsoft Copilot Chat. The most visible category. Risk: what staff type or paste into these tools - client data, financials, internal strategy - entering a public model. Requires: a clear acceptable use policy and staff training on what can and cannot go in.

🔧

Tier 2: AI-Embedded Tools

M365 Copilot, SentinelOne AI, your CRM, your PMS. The category most organizations miss. Risk: the tool already has access to your data - AI is processing emails, meetings, and files without your staff realizing it's happening. Requires: a data access audit for each tool, not just an acceptable use policy.

⚡

Tier 3: Agentic AI

Autonomous agents, workflow automation with AI, coding assistants like Cursor. The highest-risk category. Risk: AI that takes actions - writes code, modifies records, executes workflows - without requiring human review at each step. Requires: explicit approval gates and defined boundaries before any deployment.

AI Governance Program

Policy that travels with your people

An AI governance framework isn't a technology document. It's a set of decisions - about acceptable use, data handling, staff responsibilities, and accountability - written clearly enough that every employee understands their obligations before they open a prompt window.

The full program covers six pillars: AI strategy and roadmap, governance and ethics, data privacy and security, tool integration and automation, model monitoring, and regulatory compliance. Not every organization needs all six at once - the readiness assessment determines where to start and what to sequence.

Structured against the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) and delivered in three phases: Establish (inventory and policy, first 60 days), Assess (gap analysis and compliance mapping, 60 to 180 days), and Operate (monitoring cadence and ongoing governance, 180 days forward). Sized to your actual risk profile, not a framework template.
